Cosmogony and Cosmology

Philip K. Dick · 1978
A 1978 philosophical essay by Philip K. Dick in which he develops his concept of reality as a projected framework generated by an artifact he calls Zebra, a computerlike teaching machine. Dick argues that this artifact projects our world as a mirror for its maker (the Urgrund, a term borrowed from Jakob Boehme, 1616) to achieve self-awareness and self-knowledge. The essay is a key document in understanding Dick's late metaphysical and theological thinking, closely related to his ongoing Exegesis project.

How to Build a Universe That Doesn't Fall Apart Two Days Later

Philip K. Dick · 1978
A celebrated 1978 essay and speech by Philip K. Dick in which he explores questions of reality, authenticity, and what it means for things and people to be real versus fake. Originally delivered as a speech, Dick weaves together personal anecdotes, philosophical reflection, and science-fictional speculation. He discusses Disneyland, fascism, and the nature of the universe with characteristic wit and depth. A key primary text for understanding his philosophical worldview.
