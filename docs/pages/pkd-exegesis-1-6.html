PKD Exegesis Pages 1-6

Philip K. Dick · Primary Sources · PKD-authored

This document contains scanned images of pages 1 through 6 from Philip K. Dick's Exegesis, the sprawling private philosophical and theological journal that Dick maintained from 1974 until his death in 1982. The Exegesis is widely regarded as one of the most extraordinary documents in American literary history -- an estimated 8,000 handwritten pages in which Dick attempted to interpret and systematize the mystical and visionary experiences he underwent beginning in February and March of 1974 (events he referred to collectively as '2-3-74'). The text extraction presented here is garbled OCR output, rendered illegible in digital form due to the extreme difficulty of performing optical character recognition on Dick's handwritten manuscript. The characters that appear in the extracted text are artifacts of the scanning process attempting to interpret Dick's handwriting, and they do not constitute readable content. As such, the substantive content of these six pages must be accessed through the original scanned page images rather than through the text layer. These opening pages of the Exegesis are of particular scholarly significance as they represent the beginning of Dick's sustained engagement with questions about the nature of reality, divine revelation, Gnostic cosmology, and the relationship between his fiction and his lived experience. The Exegesis has been partially published in edited form, most notably in The Exegesis of Philip K. Dick (2011), edited by Pamela Jackson and Jonathan Lethem, and the Zebrapedia Project has undertaken a collaborative transcription effort. These first six pages belong to the earliest stratum of the document, produced when Dick's visionary experiences were freshest, and they represent primary source material of the highest importance for understanding the intersection of Dick's metaphysical thought and literary imagination.
