Philip K. Dick Public Appearances Audit

Scholarship

This research document provides a systematic audit of the full scope of Philip K. Dick's documented public appearances across his life and career. Drawing on biographer Paul Williams's characterization of Dick as a 'reluctant convention-goer' and the introduction to The Last Interview and Other Conversations, the document establishes that Dick's public presence was concentrated primarily between 1972 and 1982, with virtually no documented activity during the 1960s when he published most of his major works. The audit begins with Dick's first major public lecture on February 17, 1972, 'The Android and the Human,' delivered at a luncheon at the University of British Columbia's Faculty Club in Vancouver, introduced by Professor Susan Wood's English department. Dick repeated this keynote the following day at VCON 2 at the Biltmore Hotel in Vancouver, where fewer than 100 people attended and Dick made headlines by announcing he intended to remain in Canada. The document covers the July 18, 1973 recorded talk at Cal State Fullerton with Professor Willis E. McNelly, who had persuaded Dick to donate his manuscripts to CSUF's Special Collections Library. Dick's appearance as Professional Guest of Honor at Westercon 27 in Santa Barbara (July 3-7, 1974) is noted. The most extensively documented appearance is Dick's September 24, 1977 speech at the Second International Science Fiction Festival in Metz, France, where he delivered 'If You Find This World Bad, You Should See Some of the Others,' declaring that 'we are living in a computer-programmed reality.' The audit records that the speech was poorly received, with Dick speaking in monotone and the interpreter's transcript differing from his actual words due to a last-minute rewrite. The document also covers 'How to Build a Universe That Doesn't Fall Apart Two Days Later' (1978), noting it was written in speech form but never actually delivered as a lecture. Multiple radio appearances in Vancouver and Los Angeles are catalogued, as are interviews conducted at the Metz festival by Uwe Anton, Werner Fuchs, Yves Breux, and Francis Luxereau.
