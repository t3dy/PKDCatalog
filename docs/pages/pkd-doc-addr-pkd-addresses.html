Philip K. Dick: Addresses and Residences Chronology

Scholarship

This reference document catalogues every known address, residence, and significant location associated with Philip K. Dick from his birth in Chicago in 1928 through his final years in Southern California. The chronology is organized in two sections: a primary table of residential addresses with years of residence, full street addresses, and notable works or life events associated with each location, followed by a supplementary table of significant non-residential locations including conference venues, educational institutions, literary settings, and other landmarks. The residential chronology begins with Dick's birth at 7812 Emerald Avenue, Chicago, in 1928 and traces his movements through Greeley, Colorado (219 13th Street, 1930s), Washington, D.C. (3039 Macomb Street NW, 1936-1938), and a series of Berkeley addresses including 1212 Walnut Street (1938), 1711 Allston Way (late 1930s, where he attended Berkeley High School), 1931 Dwight Way (1947-1948), 2328 Telegraph Avenue (1948-1952, where he worked at Art Music Company owned by Herb Hollis), and 1126 Francisco Street (1950-1958, where he married Kleo Apostolides). Later residences include Lorraine Avenue in Point Reyes Station (where he wrote The Man in the High Castle), 707 Hacienda Way in San Rafael (the communal household that inspired A Scanner Darkly), Cornwall Avenue in Vancouver (the X-Kalay rehabilitation period), 3028 Quartz Lane in Fullerton (where he wrote A Scanner Darkly), and his final residence at 408 E. Civic Center Drive in Santa Ana (where he wrote VALIS and The Divine Invasion). The document also includes supplementary material drawn from Sherry Gotlieb's account of her correspondence with Dick and her role as a script reader at United Artists, where her synopsis of Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep? led to the book's first option. References to Patricia Warrick's scholarly work and Frank Hollander's self-published edition of Dick's wartime adolescent writings further enrich this resource as a biographical reference tool.
