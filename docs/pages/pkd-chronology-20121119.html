Philip K. Dick Extant Fiction Chronology

Frank Hollander · 2012 · Scholarship

This document is a detailed chronology of Philip K. Dick's extant completed fiction, compiled and sequenced by Frank Hollander between July and November 2012. The chronology uses the Scott Meredith Literary Agency (SMLA) submission date as its primary organizing principle, treating the date Dick first substantially finished a work and sent it to his agent as the most reliable method for structuring the reading order of his fiction. Hollander acknowledges the chronology's similarity to Lord RC's chronology published in Pink Beam (2006) but distinguishes his project as focused specifically on establishing a coherent reading order for the complete extant fiction, excluding juvenilia. The document cross-references four major biographical and bibliographic sources: Paul Williams's Only Apparently Real (1986), the Collected Stories edited by Gregg Rickman and Paul Williams (1987), Gregg Rickman's To The High Castle (1989), and Lawrence Sutin's Divine Invasions (1989). The chronology identifies three major breaks in the sequence corresponding to significant shifts in Dick's geographic location and personal life, which roughly correspond with changes in marriage. The earliest works listed include 'Stability' (written 1947 or earlier) and the novel Gather Yourselves Together (dated uncertainly to 1949-50), with a note that at the time Dick joined the SMLA he had completed one novel and was beginning his second, Voices from the Street. The SMLA sequence proper begins on July 23, 1952, with 'The Builder' and 'Meddler,' followed by a dense burst of short story submissions through 1952 and 1953, including well-known works such as 'Paycheck,' 'Colony,' 'Second Variety,' 'The Golden Man,' 'The Father-Thing,' and many others. The document provides exact SMLA receipt dates for each work and includes Hollander's own analytical notes on sequencing decisions, making it an invaluable bibliographic tool for scholars and readers seeking to understand the chronological development of Dick's literary output.
