Deleuze's Dick

Scholarship

"Deleuze's Dick" is a 32-page scholarly essay examining the philosophical connections between Gilles Deleuze's thought and Philip K. Dick's fiction. The paper represents a significant strand of contemporary Dick scholarship that reads his work through the lens of French post-structuralist and post-Deleuzian philosophy.

Deleuze himself referenced Dick's work, and his philosophical concepts of difference, repetition, the virtual, and the fold have proven remarkably productive for understanding Dick's literary explorations of unstable realities, multiple timelines, and fragmented subjectivities. The essay contributes to a growing body of scholarship that positions Dick not merely as a science fiction writer but as a thinker whose literary intuitions anticipated and parallel some of the most sophisticated philosophical developments of the twentieth century.

This paper joins works by David Lapoujade, James Burton, and others in demonstrating the deep affinity between Dick's fictional worlds and continental philosophical traditions, particularly the Deleuzian emphasis on processes of becoming, the constructive nature of reality, and the creative power of difference.
