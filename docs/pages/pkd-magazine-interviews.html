PKD Magazine Interviews: Compiled Transcripts and Analysis

Various; compiled by unknown editor · Interviews

This 167-page compilation assembles Philip K. Dick's magazine interviews alongside related scholarly analysis and bibliographic commentary. The document opens with an examination of 'Three Sci-Fi Authors View the Future,' published in Scholastic Voice on January 17, 1974, which featured Dick alongside Kurt Vonnegut and Michael Crichton. The compiler traces the piece's bibliographic history through Daniel J. H. Levack's PKD bibliography, the Stephensen-Payne and Benson updated bibliography Philip Kindred Dick: Metaphysical Conjurer, and Frank Bertrand's research, noting discrepancies in how the piece has been classified -- as nonfiction article, as interview, or as something in between. A letter from Dick to Ursula K. Le Guin dated February 2, 1974, in which Dick describes being 'interviewed by Voice, a magazine used in school,' is cited as evidence of the classification confusion. The compiler clarifies that the piece consists of short essays with pull quotes rather than a formal question-and-answer format. The Scholastic Voice section on Dick discusses The Three Stigmata of Palmer Eldritch, The Man in the High Castle, and Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?, and includes Dick's reflections on science fiction's role as a guide for coping with the present environment rather than escapism. Dick expresses his view that humanity's destiny is tragic but can be met with dignity, courage, and humor. The compilation also includes 'Horselover Fat and The New Messiah,' a 1981 interview by John Boonstra published in the Hartford Advocate, in which Dick discusses the publication history of VALIS, explaining that he wrote two completely different versions of the novel and that the first version appears within the published book as the movie the characters watch. Dick reveals that the voice he associated with a 'transcendentally rational mind' had not spoken to him since writing The Divine Invasion. This compilation is a significant resource for understanding Dick's public self-presentation and his evolving views on his own work across the final decade of his life.
