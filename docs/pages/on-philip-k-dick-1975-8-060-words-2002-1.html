Philip K. Dick's Opus: Artifice as Refuge and World View (1975)

Darko Suvin · 1975 (chapter repr. 2002) · Scholarship

This is Chapter 9 of a scholarly volume, originally published in 1975, presenting a foundational critical analysis of Philip K. Dick's literary opus. The author divides Dick's writing career into three main periods with detailed close readings of individual novels.

The first period (1952-62) is characterized as an apprenticeship, beginning with short stories (1952-56) and early novels from Solar Lottery to Vulcan's Hammer (1955-60), culminating in the mature polyphony of The Man in the High Castle (1962). The second period (1962-65) is identified as a "high plateau" comprising masterpieces including Martian Time-Slip and Dr. Bloodmoney, as well as The Three Stigmata of Palmer Eldritch, described as a "flawed but powerful near-masterpiece." The third period (1966-74) is characterized as "in many ways a falling off," marked by a turning from productive creative tension.

At 60 pages, this chapter represents one of the earliest and most substantial pieces of academic literary criticism devoted to Dick. The periodization framework it establishes has been influential in subsequent Dick scholarship, particularly the identification of the 1962-65 period as Dick's creative zenith. Published in 1975 during Dick's lifetime, it is a foundational document in the academic study of his work.
